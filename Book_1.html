CHAPTER 1: The Mortal Rival

Once upon a time, in a faraway land, there lived a great king with three daughters. The youngest, Psyche, was so breathtakingly beautiful that men from far and wide came to marvel at her, neglecting their temples and offerings to the goddess Venus.

 

Venus: This mortal dare to rival my beauty? My altars are abandoned because of her! I will not stand for it.

 

The goddess of love, enraged with jealousy, called upon her son, Cupid, the god of love, passion and fertility.

 

Venus: Cupid, you must make her suffer. Shoot her with your arrow and make her fall in love with the most disgusting, horrible wretch. (Close) (Open)

 

Obedient as he was to his mother, Cupid went to Psyche’s place. Yet, as he looked upon her, a strange thing happened— as if he bad shot one of his arrows into his own heart.

Cupid (softly): She is… so beautiful, unlike any mortal I have seen.

 

Venus was confident that Cupid will be able to ruin Psyche’s life but Pysche did not fall in love with the horrible wretch, and Cupid fell in love with her.

CHAPTER 2: The Oracle’s Fate

Though admired, Psyche found no husband. Men worshipped her beauty, but none dared to ask for her hand or be in love with her. Her father, troubled, sought the guidance of the Oracle of Apollo on how to get her a husband. Cupid beforehand went to Apollo and begged for help.

 

Apollo (voice-over): Take her to the highest mountain, and she will be left alone. There, she will be wed to a husband not of mortal kind. A terrible winged serpent shall claim her as his bride.

 

Psyche, dressed as a bride to meet her doom, stood bravely on the mountain’s peak. The king and queen, weeping, obeyed. Psyche felt that her beauty is a curse and was her tribulation was about to end. The wind howled around her, yet instead of death, she felt herself lifted into the air by the gentle Zephyr, the sweetest wind

 

Zephyr carried her to a magnificent palace, gleaming with gold and silver, fit for the home of a

god.

 

Where am I? This place is wonderful and beautiful, yet… I see no one.

CHAPTER 3: Love in Shadows

Psyche was not happy not seeing her husband. Night after night, she fell deeper in love with the

CHAPTER 4: Trials of the Soul

Devastated, Psyche wandered the earth searching for her lost love and never gave up. Meanwhile, Cupid went to his mother Venus and have his wound care of, but she was mad on what happened to her son.

 

Psyche: Great goddess, have mercy. Grant me a chance to prove myself worthy of Cupid. I must go to the Gods and ask help.

 

Venus (evil laughing): You? Worthy? My son almost died because of you! Very well—if you wish to see him again, you must serve me. You break my son’s trust and love; therefore, you must suffer.

 

Venus gave Psyche impossible tasks—sorting a mountain of seeds before dawn, gathering golden wool from deadly rams, fetching water from the River Styx. But each time, nature took pity on her.

Finally, Venus gave her the most perilous task of all—descend to the Underworld and carry a box to fill it with some of Proserpine’s beauty.

Way to Hades, river of death, she must give the ferry man a penny to take her across. Cerberus, three-headed dog. Box for beauty charm, but box is empty.

 

Psyche: Just a little for myself… surely, it would not hurt.

 

But as she opened the door, a deadly languor took her, a heavy sleep came upon her and she collapsed, lost to the world.

CHAPTER 5: The Divine Union

At last, Cupid, recovered from his wound, could bear it no longer. He flew to Psyche and brushed sleep from her eyes.

 

Cupid (with action): My love, I will not lose you again. (Pricked with arrow and scolded her why she breaks her promise and ask her to bring Proserpine’s box to his mother and all will be well.

 

Psyche (softly): Cupid… you came for me.

 

Cupid: Always, Psyche. Always.

 

He carried her to Mount Olympus and pleaded before Jupiter himself. Jupiter called a full assembly of the gods and announced to all that Cupid and Psyche, now immortal, are eternally married

 

Jupiter (voice over) Very well. Psyche shall drink the ambrosia of the gods. Venus, do you still object?

Venus (huffing): A goddess is a far more fitting

 

match for my son and I do not object. (hugs and welcomes Psyche)

 

And so, Psyche was made immortal, and she and Cupid were joined in divine marriage. Love and the soul, forever entwined, never to part again.

 

And thus, their tale lives on, whispered by the stars, reminding us that when we love, it should be with all of heart and soul, when tested, only grows stronger.
